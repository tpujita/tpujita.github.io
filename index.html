Projects

Below is a collection of science projects I have completed.

  • Got Antioxidants? Using Chemiluminescence to Study the Effect of Adding Milk or Sugar on the Antioxidant Level of Tea

    This project, which I completed in 6th grade, is about how milk and sugar affect the antioxidant levels of green and black tea. I used three different increments of each type of additive: 10, 20, and 30 mL of milk; and 1⁄4, 1⁄2, and 1 tsp of sugar. I tested the amount of antioxidants using a chemiluminescence reaction and a light-detecting circuit. Because antioxidants inhibit this reaction, I was able to see relative antioxidant levels based off of light levels. To get a quantitative measure of the light intensity, I made a light-detecting circuit, which measured light intensity in voltage. Using this data, I found that adding more milk or sugar to both green and black tea leads to more light intensity, which represents lower antioxidant levels.

  • A Green, Low-Cost Adsorbent for the Removal of Dye from Aqueous Solutions

    The purpose of this project, which I conducted in 7th grade, was to test the efficiency of spent tea leaves (STL) as a low cost adsorbent of dye using a column adsorption filter system. In this experiment, the effect of flow rate, adsorbent dose, and initial dye concentration on the adsorption of methylene blue by STL was studied. The variables were tested by a column adsorption filter system, and samples were analyzed using a homemade spectrophotometer. Maximum dye removal efficiency of up to 99% was attained depending on the adsorption conditions. Maximum adsorption capacity of 95.41 mg/g was also obtained. In conclusion, a column filter system using STL can be applied in the real world for the removal of dyes from industrial wastewaters as a green, cost-effective alternative to other expensive treatment methods.

  • A Green, Low-Cost Solution for the Removal and Recovery of Dye from Industrial Wastewater (A Second Year Study)

    The purpose of this project (a continuation of my 7th project that I conducted in 8th grade) was to study the efficiency of spent tea leaves (STL) as a green, low-cost solution for the removal and recovery of dye from wastewater. The effect of temperature on adsorption, and pH on adsorption and desorption of dye by STL was studied to find the maximum adsorption capacity and percent desorbed by performing continuous studies. Maximum adsorption capacity increased by 139% from a single cycle to continuous studies. Desorption studies led to 94.44% recovery of dye. Therefore, this column adsorption system using STL has the potential to be scaled to an industrial level as an effective, low-cost, eco-friendly solution for wastewater remediation.

  • Comparing Correlation between Color and Metallicity in Cluster vs Non-Cluster Galaxies

    The purpose of this project, which I conducted in 9th grade, was to determine correlations between color and metallicity in both cluster and non-cluster galaxies and compare them. The best-fit line equations for these correlations could then be used to calculate metallicity from a photometric g-r (color) reading. RA (right ascension) and DEC (declination) of member galaxies of the Virgo Cluster were retrieved from the ViZieR Database. Coordinates were converted from sexagesimal to decimal degrees using Python. These coordinates were plugged into SDSS through a CasJobs SQL Query to retrieve g, r, H Alpha flux, and Nii flux, as well as their errors. Color was expressed as g-r, and metallicity was calculated using a metallicity calibration formula from Pettini & Pagel 2004. Data for non-cluster galaxies was retrieved with a radial search at RA and DEC of (0, 0). Both correlations show that g-r varies directly with metallicity.
